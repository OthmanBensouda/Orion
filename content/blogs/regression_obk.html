AirBnB analytics - Beijing

Introduction

The following project focuses on the analysis of an AirBnB dataset. A data cleaning has been firstly conducted to ensure reliable data. The output of the is a multivariate regression model that predicts the total cost of two people to stay at an AirBnB property in Beijing for 4 nights.

Executive summary

The dataset obtained from airBnB contained a vast amount of information which allows for the development of a broad range of insights into the operations of airBnB in Beijing. My group and I eliminated over half the columns in the dataset that were not directly related to our task of building a model for the total price of a airBnB over 4 nights. We also had to convert a number of the columns into workable data as many numeric values were stored as strings. After building a workable dataset, the data was explored and visualizations were created to further develop an understanding of the data. We observed that there were dramatically different average prices depending on the neighbourhood. Interestingly, the properties in the city centre were not the most expensive as one might initially assume. Instead, Huajiou, which is located ~100km outside of the city centre, was the most expensive district. We realized this was due to it’s proximity to the Great Wall, a highly popular tourist destination. Additionally the property information was overlaid onto a map of Beijing to provide a visual aid to understand the distribution of AirBnb properties and their prices throughout Beijing. In order to narrow down the variables to include in the regression models, correlation matrices were built. It was discovered there were 3 sets of variables that were highly intercorrelated. These were related to rating scores; number of reviews; and accommodation size. When we further examined these groups it was discovered the group related to accommodation size was significantly correlated with the price while the other two had very little correlation. It was also found that the correlations were even higher when the host is a superhost. The regression model was developed through iteration and the final model’s independent variables were Max accommodation, neighbourhood, Number of Bedrooms, Room Type, Rating which has a R2 value of 0.509 Our final regression model was used to predict the price for 2 people to rent for 4 nights from a property with a rating of greater than 90% and more than 10 reviews. The predicted price was ¥1,192 with a 95% confidence interval from ¥1,166 to ¥1,218.

Regression Model

We would like to know what would be the price for two people to stay at an Airbnb location for four nights. In order to make this prediction, we will run a linear regression with the price for four nights as the Y variable.

Our goal is to come up with a model that explains the variability of the price for four nights. We will run various regressions in order to reach a model with the following characteristics :

  • The model has a high adjusted R-squared
  • The model has statistically significant estimators
  • The model’s estimators are not collinear
  • The model respects L-I-N-E assumptions of linear regression

We first plot our variable price_4_nights in order to study its distribution.

#We plot the distribution of price_4_nights
ggplot(beijing_cleanbase,aes(x=price_4_nights)) + geom_histogram() + theme_bw() + labs(title="The distribution of the price for 4 nights is right skewed", subtitle= "Distribution of the price for four nights", x="Price for four nights", y="Count")

We notice that the distribution of price_4_nights is right skewed, indicating that we have a few outliers. Moreover, using price_4_nights could result in heteroskedasticity of the residuals. We could reduce the influence of the outliers and improve homoskedasticity by using a log scale, which is a more robust approach. Thus, we will use price_4_nights_log, which seems more symmetrical.

#We remove these 10 rows
beijing_cleanbase <- beijing_cleanbase %>%
  filter(price_4_nights!=0)
#We plot the distribution of price_4_nights_log
ggplot(beijing_cleanbase,aes(x=price_4_nights_log)) + geom_histogram() + theme_bw() + labs(title="The distribution of log(price_4_nights) is normally distributed", subtitle="Distribution of log(price_4_nights)",x="log(price_4_nights)", y="count")

This model has indeed many problems : * The linearity assumption is not respected, as there seems to be a pattern in the residuals vs fitted plot. * Residuals do not follow a normal distribution * There are points with very high leverage that can influence our estimators. * The variability of the random disturbance is different across the variable, which is a sign of heteroskedasticity. This indicates biases in our estimates.

It wouldn’t be relevant to interpret our coefficients in this model since they are most likely biased. Note that the model automatically used prop_type_apartment as the basis to avoid multicollinearity.

All these variables only increase the R squared by 0.09, which is not significant at all. There is a tradeoff between having a simple model and having a precise model. The small increase in R squared does not justify the added complexity generated by these 7 new variables. Thus, we will decide to leave them out of our model.

For now, our best model has been model11.

It explains 50.9% of the variability in the price for 4 nights. The VIFs are all below 5, indicating that there is no sign of multicollinearity. All our coefficients are significant at the 0.1% level.

However, since we are great statisticians, we must also criticize our model. We have tried to correct for outliers in our dataset but some may still be troublesome as they have an impact on the assumptions of linear regression. Some points have a high leverage, residuals do not follow perfectly a normal distribution and there are some minors sign of heteroskedasticity. All in all, we believe that our model has a high explanatory power but that some of our coefficients may be slightly biased.
